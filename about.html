Customer Success background. Not a software engineer by training. Someone who learned by building something real.

NeoPadio started as a personal frustration: we were building something real and kept losing track of what we'd learned. So we built the workspace we needed, and kept going because the product kept teaching us.

Every feature was built because we wanted to understand how learning actually works—not just make another productivity tool.

What we stand for
01

Understanding over storage

We don't believe knowledge should disappear into folders. Every memory, note, book, article, and video should help you build understanding, not just fill up storage.

02

Everything you learn, in one place

Learning doesn't happen in one format. Ideas come from conversations, books, articles, videos, and quick notes. NeoPadio brings them together into one connected knowledge workspace.

03

AI grounded in your knowledge

AI should work from what you've actually saved, not generic internet answers. Every summary, explanation, and conversation is grounded in your own notes, books, articles, videos, and memories.

04

Learn once. Remember forever.

Great ideas shouldn't be forgotten after a week. NeoPadio helps you revisit, connect, and retain what matters so your knowledge compounds over time instead of getting lost.

June 28, 2026

We use NeoPadio to build NeoPadio

Every technical concept we learn (REST APIs, auth, database patterns) goes into NeoPadio as a note. Then we use Feynman Mode to check if we actually understand it. The product is its own best test.

Meta
June 14, 2026

Why we started with Memory, not Notes

Notes felt like the obvious starting point. But memory capture (quick, voice-first, automatic analysis) is where the real value is. The moment of capturing is the highest-intent moment. We should design for that first.

Decision
May 30, 2026

What a Customer Success background actually gives you

You spend years watching where users get confused and why. You develop a very sharp instinct for where a product will break down before it's even built. That turned out to be more valuable than we expected.

Reflection
May 10, 2026

The hardest product decision: what to leave out

Dashboards. Streaks. Progress charts. We've built and removed all of them. Each time, the product got calmer and more focused. The right call is almost always to remove, not add. That took a while to learn.
